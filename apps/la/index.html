2-Dimensional Case

First, let's consider a system of two linear equations with two unknowns. Namely, \(2x-y=0\) and \(-x+2y=3\) . We can represent these equations graphically as:

This same set of equations may be represented by one matrix form linear algebra equation, \(A\overrightarrow{x}=\overrightarrow{b}\), as: $$ \left[ \begin{array}{cc} 2 & -1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{array} \right] \left[ \begin{array}{c} x \\ y \end{array} \right]= \left[ \begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 3 \end{array} \right] $$ The column vector representation, \(x\overrightarrow{w}+y\overrightarrow{v}=\overrightarrow{b}\), of the same linear algebra equation is: $$ x\left[ \begin{array}{c} 2 \\ -1\end{array} \right] + y\left[ \begin{array}{c} -1 \\ 2 \end{array} \right]= \left[ \begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 3 \end{array} \right] $$ The column vector form expresses the right hand vector as a linear combination of the left columns shown in the linear algebra equation. We can represent this linear algebra column vector form equation geometrically as:
Notice how the solution of \(x=1\) and \(y=2\) is reflected in the multiples of vectors \(\overrightarrow{w}\) and \(\overrightarrow{v}\) in the above graph required to finish at the point \((0,3)\). This shows that \(1\overrightarrow{w}+2\overrightarrow{v}=\overrightarrow{b}\).

As long as the left side vectors \(\overrightarrow{w}\) and \(\overrightarrow{v}\) are non-colinear, the vector space defined by letting \(x\) and \(y\) be any value covers the entire \(xy\) plane. Inotherwords, for every point on the \(xy\) plane, there exists a pair of values for \(x\) and \(y\) that will define linear combinations of the left side vectors finishing at that point since vectors \(\overrightarrow{w}\) and \(\overrightarrow{v}\) are non-colinear.

3-Dimensional Case

MATLAB Example

Copy the following code and overwrite the current code in the demo.m file within the Octave MATLAB environment:
A = [ 1 2 4 4; 1 4 5 2; 2 7 8 -1]
B = rref(A)

The preceding example is equivalent to finding the reduced row eschelon form of the following augmented matrix: $$ \newenvironment{amatrix}[1]{% \left(\begin{array}{@{}*{#1}{ccc}|c@{}} }{% \end{array}\right) } \begin{amatrix}{3} 1 & 2 & 4 & 4 \\ 1 & 4 & 5 & 2 \\ 2 & 7 & 8 & -1 \end{amatrix} $$ The same example is also a way of solving the following equation: $$ \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & 2 & 4\\ 1 & 4 & 5\\ 2 & 7 & 8 \end{array} \right] \left[ \begin{array}{c} x \\ y \\ z\end{array} \right]= \left[ \begin{array}{c} 4 \\ 2 \\ -1 \end{array} \right] $$ Now copy/paste and execute the following in the Octave MATLAB environment:
A = [ 1 2 4 ; 1 4 5 ; 2 7 8]
I = inv(A)
b = [4; 2; -1]
I*b

This example is equivalent to solving a matrix linear algebra equation by left multiplying both sides by the inverse of matrix 'A', \(A^{-1}\), as in: $$ A\overrightarrow{x}=\overrightarrow{b} $$ $$ A^{-1}A\overrightarrow{x}=A^{-1}\overrightarrow{b} $$ $$ \overrightarrow{x}=A^{-1}\overrightarrow{b} $$ The computation of the reduced row eschelon form, MATLAB rref(), is considerably faster than the inverse matrix based computation. The reduced row eschelon form function used by MATLAB is performed using the Gaussian-Jordan Elimation algorithm. Computational speed is very important with real-world data which often deals with huge and complex data sets. Huge, because these data sets are often terabytes in size or larger, and complex because real-world quantities are often dependent on a large number of parameters (Think: parameters equal dimensions.).

Application

Some applications of linear algebra involve systems of linear equations where the interdependence of rows within the matrices is static as in the form \(A\overrightarrow{x}=\overrightarrow{b}\). Many other 'dynamic' systems have interdependence between rows that is time and/or space dependent. A fundamental understanding of calculus is required to adequately define dynamic linear equations of the form \(A\overrightarrow{x}=\overrightarrow{\lambda}\).
